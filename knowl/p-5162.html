I have given a very formal and detailed proof of this theorem, to show how it can be done and to show how induction plays a role in many proofs about grammars. However, a more informal proof of the theorem would probably be acceptable and might even be more convincing. To show that \(L\SUB L(G)\text{,}\) we could just note that the derivation \(S\YIELDS aSb\YIELDS a^2Sb^2\YIELDS\)\(\cdots\)\(\YIELDS a^nSb^n\YIELDS a^nb^n\) demonstrates that \(a^nb^n\in L\text{.}\) On the other hand, it is clear that every derivation for this grammar must be of this form, so every string in \(L(G)\) is of the form \(a^nb^n\text{.}\)
