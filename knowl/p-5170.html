Suppose that the first and last characters of \(w\) are different. Then \(w\) is either of the form \(axb\) or of the form \(bxa\text{,}\) for some string \(x\text{.}\) Let’s assume that \(w\) is of the form \(axb\text{.}\) (The case where \(w\) is of the form \(bxa\) is handled in a similar way.) Since \(w\) has the same number of \(a\text{'s}\) and \(b\text{'s}\) and since \(x\) has one fewer \(a\) than \(w\) and one fewer \(b\) than \(w\text{,}\) \(x\) must also have the same number of \(a\text{'s}\) as \(b\text{'s}\text{.}\) That is \(x\in L\text{.}\) But \(|x|=|w|-2 \lt k\text{,}\) so by the induction hypothesis, \(x\in L(G)\text{.}\) So we have \(S\;\YIELDSTAR x\text{.}\) By Theorem 17.5.2, we get then \(aSb\;\YIELDSTAR axb\text{.}\) Combining this with the fact that \(S\YIELDS aSb\text{,}\) we get that \(S\;\YIELDSTAR axb\text{,}\) that is, \(S\;\YIELDSTAR w\text{.}\) This proves that \(w\in L(G)\text{.}\)
