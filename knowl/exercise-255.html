Exercise 3.

Let \(A = \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & -1 \\ \end{array} \right)\text{,}\) \(B= \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 3 & 7 & 6 \\ 2 & -1 & 5 \\ \end{array} \right)\text{,}\) and \(C= \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & -2 & 4 \\ 7 & 1 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right)\text{.}\) Compute the following as efficiently as possible by using any of the Laws of Matrix Algebra:
  1. \(\displaystyle A B + A C\)
  2. \(\displaystyle A^{-1}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle A(B + C)\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \left(A^2\right)^{-1}\)
  5. \(\displaystyle (C + B)^{-1}A^{-1}\)
Answer.
  1. \(\displaystyle AB+AC=\left( \begin{array}{ccc} 21 & 5 & 22 \\ -9 & 0 & -6 \\ \end{array} \right)\)
  2. Calculating determinate first using Definition 7.2.7:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{cc} \lvert A \rvert & = 1(-1) - 2(0)\\ & = -1 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Calculate inverse matrix using Theorem 7.2.9:
    \begin{equation*} A^{-1}= \frac{1}{-1}\left( \begin{array}{cc} -1 & -2 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right) = \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & -1 \\ \end{array} \right)=A \end{equation*}
  3. \(A(B+C)=A B+ A C = \) same as part a.
  4. \(\left(A^2\right)^{-1}=(AA)^{-1}\\\) By part b: \(= (A^{-1}A)=I^{-1}=I\)
  5. \((C + B)^{-1}\) does not exist because \((C+B)\) is not a square matrix.
