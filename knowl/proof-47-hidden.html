We will only present the proof of this theorem for the direct product of two groups. Some slight revisions can be made to produce a proof for any number of factors.
Stating that the direct product of two groups is a group is a short way of saying that if \(\left[G_1; \bullet_1\right]\) and \(\left[G_2; \bullet_2\right]\) are groups, then \(\left[G_1\times G_2;\bullet\right]\) is also a group, where \(\bullet\) is the componentwise operation on \(G_1\times G_2\text{.}\) Associativity of \(\bullet\) : If \(a, b, c \in G_1\times G_2\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{split} a \bullet (b \bullet c) & =\left(a_1,a_2\right)\bullet\left(\left(b_1,b_2\right)\bullet\left(c_1,c_2\right)\right)\\ & =\left(a_1,a_2\right)\bullet\left(b_1\bullet_1c_1,b_2\bullet_2c_2\right)\\ & = \left(a_1\bullet_1\left(b_1\bullet_1c_1\right),a_2\bullet_2\left(b_2\bullet_2c_2\right)\right)\\ & = \left(\left(a_1\bullet_1b_1\right)\bullet_1c_1,\left(a_2\bullet_2b_2\right)\bullet_2c_2\right)\\ & = \left(a_1\bullet_1b_1,a_2\bullet_2b_2\right)\bullet\left(c_1,c_2\right)\\ & = \left(\left(a_1,a_2\right)\bullet\left(b_1,b_2\right)\right)\bullet\left(c_1,c_2\right)\\ & = (a \bullet b)\bullet c\\ \end{split} \end{equation*}
Notice how the associativity property hinges on the associativity in each factor. An identity for \(\bullet\text{:}\) As you might expect, if \(e_1\) and \(e_2\) are identities for \(G_1\) and \(G_2\text{,}\) respectively, then \(e = \left(e_1, e _2 \right)\) is the identity for \(G_1 \times G_2\text{.}\) If \(a \in G_1\times G_2\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{split} a \bullet e &=\left(a_1,a_2\right)\bullet \left(e_1, e _2 \right)\\ & = \left(a_1\bullet_1e_1,a_2\bullet_2 e _2\right)\\ & = \left(a_1,a_2\right) = a \end{split} \end{equation*}
Similarly, \(e \bullet a = a\text{.}\)
Inverses in \(G_1\times G_2\text{:}\) The inverse of an element is determined componentwise \(a^{-1}= \left(a_1,a_2\right){}^{-1}=\left(a_1{}^{-1},a_2{}^{-1}\right)\text{.}\) To verify, we compute \(a \bullet a^{-1}\) :
\begin{equation*} \begin{split} a \bullet a^{-1} &=\left(a_1,a_2\right)\bullet\left(a_1{}^{-1},a_2{}^{-1}\right)\\ & =\left(a_1\bullet_1a_1{}^{-1},a_2\bullet_2a_2{}^{-1}\right)\\ & = \left(e_1, e _2 \right) =e \end{split} \end{equation*}
Similarly, \(a^{-1} \bullet a=e\text{.}\)
