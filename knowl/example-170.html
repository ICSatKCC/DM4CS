Example 11.1.13. Putting a Matrix into Row Reduced Echelon Form.

Suppose
\begin{equation*} A = \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4\\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 6\\ 4 \amp 4 \amp 4 \amp 10\\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
Where are the pivot positions and pivot columns?
Solution.
Replace the second row by -3 times the first added to the second. This yields
\begin{equation*} A = \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4\\ 0 \amp -4 \amp -8 \amp -6\\ 4 \amp 4 \amp 4 \amp 10\\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
This is not in reduced echelon form so replace the bottom row by -4 times the top row added to the bottom. This yields
\begin{equation*} A = \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4\\ 0 \amp -4 \amp -8 \amp -6\\ 0 \amp -4 \amp -8 \amp -6\\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
This is still not in reduced echelon form. Replace the bottom row by -1 times the middle row added to the bottom. This yields
\begin{equation*} A = \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4\\ 0 \amp -4 \amp -8 \amp -6\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
which is in echelon form, although not in reduced echelon form. Therefore, the pivot positions in the original matrix are the locations corresponding to the first row and first column and the second row and second columns as shown in the following:
\begin{equation*} A = \left( \begin{array}{cccc} \boxed{1} \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4\\ 3 \amp \boxed{2} \amp 1 \amp 6\\ 4 \amp 4 \amp 4 \amp 10\\ \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
Thus the pivot columns in the matrix are the first two columns.
