As a simple example, consider a grammar that has three production rules:
\begin{gather*} S\PRODUCES aS\\ S\PRODUCES bS \\ S\PRODUCES b \end{gather*}
In this example, \(S\) is the only non-terminal symbol, and the terminal symbols are \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Starting from the string \(S\text{,}\) we can apply any of the three rules of the grammar to produce either \(aS\) , \(bS\text{,}\) or \(b\text{.}\) Since the string \(b\) contains no non-terminals, we see that \(b\) is one of the strings in the language generated by this grammar. The strings \(aS\) and \(bS\) are not in that language, since they contain the non-terminal symbol \(S\text{,}\) but we can continue to apply production rules to these strings. From \(aS\text{,}\) for example, we can obtain \(aaS\text{,}\) \(abS\text{,}\) or \(ab\text{.}\) From \(abS\text{,}\) we go on to obtain \(abaS\text{,}\) \(abbS\text{,}\) or \(abb\text{.}\) The strings \(ab\) and \(abb\) are in the language generated by the grammar.
