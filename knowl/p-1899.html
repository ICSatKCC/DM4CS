  1. Assume that \(x\in A\) (condition of the conditional conclusion \(A \subseteq C\)). Since \(A \subseteq B\text{,}\) \(x\in B\) by the definition of \(\subseteq\text{.}\) \(B\subseteq C\) and \(x\in B\) implies that \(x\in C\text{.}\) Therefore, if \(x\in A\text{,}\) then \(x\in C\text{.}\) \(\square\)
  2. (Proof that \(A -B \subseteq A\cap \overline{B}\)) Let \(x\) be in \(A - B\text{.}\) Therefore, x is in \(A\text{,}\) but it is not in B; that is,\(\text{ }x \in A\) and \(x \in \overline{B} \Rightarrow x\in A\cap \overline{B}\text{.}\) \(\square\)
  3. \((\Rightarrow )\)Assume that \(A \subseteq B\) and \(A \subseteq C\text{.}\) Let \(x\in A\text{.}\) By the two premises,\(x\in B\) and \(x\in C\text{.}\) Therefore, by the definition of intersection, \(x\in B\cap C\text{.}\) \(\square\)
  4. \((\Rightarrow )\)(Indirect) Assume that \(\overline{B}\) is not a subset of \(\overline{A}\) . Therefore, there exists \(x\in \overline{B}\) that does not belong to \(\overline{A}\text{.}\) \(x \notin \overline{A} \Rightarrow x \in A\text{.}\) Therefore, \(x\in A\) and \(x\notin B\text{,}\) a contradiction to the assumption that \(A\subseteq B\text{.}\) \(\square\)
  5. \((\Rightarrow )\) Assume that \((x,y) \in A\times C\text{.}\) Then \(x \in A\) and \(y \in C\) by definition of Cartesian product. Since \(A \subseteq B\text{,}\) \(x\in B\) by the definition of \(\subseteq\text{.}\) Then \((x,y) \in B\times C\) by definition of Cartesian product. Therefore, \(A\times C \subseteq B\times C\) by the definition of \(\subseteq\text{.}\) \(\square\)
