    Section 15.1 Strings

    In computer science courses, we discuss many algorithms and computational models that manipulate sequences: one-dimensional arrays, linked lists, blocks of text, walks in graphs, sequences of executed instructions, and so on. Ultimately the input and output of any algorithm must be representable as a finite string of symbols‚Äîthe raw contents of some contiguous portion of the computer's memory. Reasoning about computation requires reasoning about strings.
    This section lists several formal definitions and formal induction proofs related to strings. These definitions and proofs are intentionally much more detailed than normally used in practice‚Äîmost people's intuition about strings is fairly accurate‚Äîbut the extra precision is necessary for any sort of formal proof. It may be helpful to think of this material as part of the ‚Äúassembly language‚Äù of theoretical computer science. We normally think about computation at a much higher level of abstraction, but ultimately every argument must ‚Äúcompile‚Äù down to these (and similar) definitions.
    But the actual definitions and theorems are not the point. The point of playing with this material is to get some experience working with formal/mechanical definitions and proofs, especially inductive definitions and recursive proofs. Or should I say recursive definitions and inductive proofs? Whatever, they're the same thing. Strings are a particularly simple and convenient playground for induction/recursion; which you will see many more of in an Algorithms course. When you read this section, don't just look at the content of the definitions and proofs; pay close attention to their structure and the process for creating them.

    Subsection 15.1.1 Strings

    Definition 15.1.1. Alphabet.

    Fix an arbitrary finite set \(\Sigma\) called the alphabet; the individual elements of \(\Sigma\) are called symbols or characters. As a notational convention, this chapter will always use lower-case letters near the start of the English alphabet \((a, b, c, . . . )\) as symbol variables, and never as explicit symbols. For explicit symbols, we will use upper-case letters \((A, B, C, . . . )\text{,}\) digits \((0, 1,2, . . . )\text{,}\) or other symbols \((\diamond, \$, \#, \cdot, . . . )\) that are clearly distinguishable from variables.

    Definition 15.1.2. String.

    A string (or word) over \(\Sigma\) is a finite sequence of zero or more symbols from \(\Sigma\text{.}\) Formally, a string \(w\) over \(\Sigma\) is defined recursively as either
    • the empty string, denoted by the Greek letter \(\epsilon\) (epsilon), or
    • an ordered pair \((a, x)\text{,}\) where \(a\) is a symbol in \(\Sigma\) and \(x\) is a string over \(\Sigma\text{.}\)
    We normally write either \(a \cdot x\) or simply \(ax\) to denote the ordered pair \((a,x)\text{.}\) Similarly, we normally write explicit strings as sequences of symbols instead of nested ordered pairs; for example, STRING is convenient shorthand for the formal expression \((S, (T, (R, (I, (N, (G, \epsilon))))))\text{.}\) As a notational convention, we will use lower-case letters near the end of the English alphabet \((. . . , w, x, y, z)\) for string variables, and UPPERCASE TEXT to typeset explicit (non-empty) strings.
    The set of all strings over \(\Sigma\) is denoted\(\Sigma^*\) (pronounced ‚Äúsigma star‚Äù). It is very important to remember that every element of \(\Sigma^*\) is a finite string, although \(\Sigma^*\)itself is an infinite set containing strings of every possible finite length.

    Subsection 15.1.2 Two Recursive Functions

    Our first several proofs about strings will involve two natural functions, one giving the length of a string, the other gluing two strings together into a larger string. These two functions behave exactly the way you think they do, but if we actually want to prove anything about them, we first have to define them in a way that supports formal proofs. Because the objects on which these functions act (strings) are defined recursively, the functions must also be defined recursively.

    Definition 15.1.3. String Length.

    The length \(|w|\) of a string \(w\) is the number of symbols in \(w\text{,}\) defined formally as follows:
    \begin{equation*} |w| = \begin{cases} 0, & \text{if}\ w = \epsilon \\ 1 + |x| , & \text{if}\ w = ax. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    For example, the string FIFTEEN has length 7, the string SEVEN has length 5, and the string 5 has length 1. Although they are formally different objects, we do not normally distinguish between symbols and strings of length 1.

    Definition 15.1.4. String Concatenation.

    The concatenation of two strings \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) denoted either \(x \bullet y\) or simply \(x y\text{,}\) is the unique string containing the characters of \(x\) in order, followed by the characters in \(y\) in order. For example, the string NOWHERE is the concatenation of the strings NOW and HERE; that is, NOW \(\bullet\) HERE = NOWHERE. (On the other hand, HERE \(\bullet\) NOW = HERENOW.) Formally, concatenation is defined recusively as follows:
    \begin{equation*} w \bullet z = \begin{cases} z, & \text{if}\ w = \epsilon \\ a \cdot (x \bullet z) , & \text{if}\ w = ax. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    (Here we are using a larger dot \(\bullet\) to formally distinguish the operator that concatenates two arbitrary strings from the operation \(\cdot\) that builds a string from a single character and a string.)
    When we from describe the concatenation of more than two strings, we normally omit all dots and parentheses, writing \(wxyz\) instead of \((w \bullet (x \bullet y)) \bullet z\text{,}\) for example. This simplification is justified by the fact (which we will prove shortly) that the function \(\bullet\) is associative.

    Subsection 15.1.3 Induction on Strings

    Induction is the standard technique for proving statements about recursively defined objects. Hopefully you are already comfortable proving statements about natural numbers via induction, but induction is actually a far more general technique. Several different variants of induction can be used to prove statements about more general structures; here I describe the variant that I recommend (and actually use in practice). This variant follows two primary design considerations:
    • The case structure of the proof should mirror the case structure of the recursive definition. For example, if you are proving something about all strings, your proof should have two cases: Either (the base case or basis step) string \(w = \epsilon\text{,}\) or (the recursive case in the inductive step) string \(w = ax\) for some symbol \(a\) and string \(x\text{.}\)
    • The inductive hypothesis should be as strong as possible. The (strong) inductive hypothesis for statements about natural numbers is always "Assume the statement is true for all \(j\) such that \(1 \leq j \leq k\text{.}\)" Then the inductive step is done on \(k+1\text{.}\) I recommend adopting a similar inductive hypothesis for strings: ‚ÄúAssume the statement is true for all strings \(x\text{,}\) such that \(|x| \lt |w| \text{.}\) Then for the case \(w = ax\text{,}\) we have \(|x| = |w| - 1 \lt |w|\) by definition of \(|w|\text{,}\) so the inductive hypothesis applies to \(x\)
    Thus, string-induction proofs have the following structure.

    Example 15.1.5.

    Suppose we want to prove that every string is perfectly cromulent, whatever that means. Proof details are not shown but depend on the precise definition of ‚Äúperfectly cromulent‚Äù.
    Let \(w\) be an arbitrary string.
    There are two cases to consider.
    • Basis Step: The case where \(w = \epsilon\text{.}\)
      Show \(\epsilon\) is perfectly cromulent
    • Inductive Step: The case where \(w = a x\) for some symbol \(a\) and string \(x\text{.}\)
      1. Assume Inductive Hypothesis, for every string \(x\) such that \(|x| \lt |w|,\) that \(x\) is perfectly cromulent.
      2. Show \(ax\) is also perfectly cromulent, therefore \(w\) is perfectly cromulent
    Here are three canonical examples of this proof structure. When developing proofs in this style, I strongly recommend first mindlessly writing the text from the template in the example above with lots of space for each step, then filling in the actual theorem and the induction hypothesis, and only then starting to actually think.
    Many students are confused (or at least bored and distracted) by the fact that we are proving mind-bogglingly obvious facts. If you‚Äôre one of these students, try to remember that the lemmas themselves are not the point. Pay close attention to the structure of the proofs. Notice how each proof follows the template described above. Notice how every sentence of the proof follows mechanically from earlier sentences, definitions, and the rules of standard logic and arithmetic.

    Lemma 15.1.6.

    Adding nothing does nothing: For every string \(w\text{,}\) we have\(w \bullet \epsilon = w\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(w\) be an arbitrary string. There are two cases to consider.
    Basis step: Suppose \(w = \epsilon\)
    \begin{align*} w \bullet \epsilon \amp = \epsilon\bullet\epsilon \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon\\ \amp = \epsilon \amp \text{ def. of concatenation,}\\ \amp = w \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon \end{align*}
    Inductive step:
    Assume an inductive hypothesis that \(x \bullet \epsilon = x\) for every string \(x\) such that \(|x| \lt |w|\text{.}\)
    Now suppose \(w = ax\) for some symbol \(a\) and string \(x\) (\(w\) is string \(x\) with one more character, \(a\text{,}\) concatenated on to it.)
    \begin{align*} w \bullet \epsilon \amp = (a \cdot x)\bullet \epsilon \amp \text{Because } w = ax\\ \amp = a \cdot (x \bullet \epsilon) \amp \text{ by definition of concatenation} \\ \amp = a \cdot x \amp \text{ by inductive hypothesis} \\ \amp = w \amp \text{Because } w = ax \end{align*}
    Therefore we conclude that \(w \bullet \epsilon = w \, \blacksquare\)

    Lemma 15.1.7.

    Concatenation adds length: \(|w \bullet x| = |w| + |x| \text{,}\) for all strings \(w\) and \(x\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(w\) and \(x\) be arbitrary strings. There are two cases to consider.
    Basis step: Suppose \(w = \epsilon\)
    \begin{align*} |w \bullet x| \amp = |\epsilon\bullet x| \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon\\ \amp = |x| \amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = |\epsilon| + |x| \amp |\epsilon| = 0 \text{ by definition of length}, |\,| \\ \amp = |w| + |x| \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon \end{align*}
    Inductive step:
    Assume an inductive hypothesis that \(|y \bullet x| = |y| + |x|\) for every string \(y\) such that \(|y| \lt |w|\text{.}\)
    Now suppose \(w = ay\) for some symbol \(a\) and string \(x\) (\(w\) is string \(x\) with one more character, \(a\text{,}\) concatenated on to it.)
    \begin{align*} |w \bullet x| \amp = |ay \bullet x| \amp \text{Because } w = ay\\ \amp = |a \cdot (y \bullet x)| \amp \text{ by definition of } \bullet \\ \amp = 1 + |y \bullet x| \amp \text{ by definition of length}, |\,| \\ \amp = 1 + |y| + |x| \amp \text{ by inductive hypothesis}, |\,| \\ \amp = |ay| + |x| \amp \text{ by definition of length}, |\,| \\ \amp = |w| + |x| \amp \text{Because } w = ay \end{align*}
    Therefore we conclude that \(|w \bullet x| = |w| + |x| \, \blacksquare\)

    Lemma 15.1.8.

    Concatenation is associative: \((w \bullet x) \bullet y = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \text{,}\) for all strings \(w, x\) and \(y\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(w, x\) and \(y\) be arbitrary strings. There are two cases to consider.
    Basis step: Suppose \(w = \epsilon\)
    \begin{align*} (w \bullet x) \bullet y \amp = (\epsilon \bullet x) \bullet y \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon\\ \amp = x \bullet y \amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = \epsilon \bullet (x \bullet y)\amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon \end{align*}
    Inductive step:
    Assume an inductive hypothesis that\((z \bullet x) \bullet y = z \bullet (x \bullet y) \) for every string \(z\) such that \(|z| \lt |w|\text{.}\)
    Now suppose \(w = az\) for some symbol \(a\) and string \(z\) (\(w\) is string \(z\) with one more character, \(a\text{,}\) concatenated on to it.)
    \begin{align*} (w \bullet x) \bullet y \amp = (az \bullet x) \bullet y \amp \text{Because } w = az\\ \amp = (a \cdot (z \bullet x)) \bullet y \amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = a \cdot ((z \bullet x)) \bullet y) \amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = a \cdot (z \bullet (x \bullet y)) \amp \text{ by the inductive hypothesis} \\ \amp = az \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{Because } w = az \end{align*}
    Therefore we conclude that \((w \bullet x) \bullet y = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \, \blacksquare\)

    Subsection 15.1.4 More Than One Path up the Mountain

    This is not the only template that one can use for induction proofs on strings. For example, we can model our case analysis on the following observation, whose easy proof we leave as an exercise (hint, hint): A string \(w \in \Sigma^*\) is non-empty if and only if either
    • \(w = a\) for some symbol \(a \in \Sigma\) (\(w\) is just one character), or
    • \(w = x \bullet y\) for some non-empty strings \(x\) and \(y\) .
    In the latter case, Lemma Lemma¬†15.1.7 implies that \(|x| \lt |w|\) and \(|y| \lt |w|\text{,}\) so in an inductive proof, we can apply to inductive hypothesis to either \(x\) or \(y\) (or even both).
    Here is a proof of Lemma Lemma¬†15.1.8 that uses this alternative recursive structure:

    Proof.

    Let \(w, x\) and \(y\) be arbitrary strings. There are three cases to consider.
    Basis step 1: Suppose \(w = \epsilon\)
    \begin{align*} (w \bullet x) \bullet y \amp = (\epsilon \bullet x) \bullet y \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon\\ \amp = x \bullet y \amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = \epsilon \bullet (x \bullet y)\amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{Because } w = \epsilon \end{align*}
    Basis step 2: Suppose \(w = a\text{,}\) \(w\) is a single symbol.
    \begin{align*} (w \bullet x) \bullet y \amp = (a \bullet x) \bullet y \amp \text{Because } w = a\\ \amp = (a \cdot x)\bullet y \amp \text{ Because } a \bullet z = a \cdot z \text{ by def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = a \cdot (x \bullet y)\amp \text{ def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = a \bullet (x \bullet y)\amp \text{ Because } a \bullet z = a \cdot z \text{ by def. of } \bullet \\ \amp = = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{Because } w = a \end{align*}
    Inductive step:
    Assume an inductive hypothesis that\((z \bullet x') \bullet y' = z \bullet (x' \bullet y') \) for all strings \(x', y'\) and \(z\) such that \(|z| \lt |w|\) .
    Now suppose \(w = u \bullet v\) for some non-empty strings \(u\) and \(v\) .
    \begin{align*} (w \bullet x) \bullet y \amp = ((u \bullet v) \bullet x) \bullet y \amp \text{Because } w = u \bullet v\\ \amp = (u \bullet (v \bullet x)) \bullet y \amp \text{ by I.H, because } |u| \lt |w| \\ \amp = u \bullet ((v \bullet x)) \bullet y) \amp \text{ by I.H, because } |u| \lt |w| \\ \amp = u \bullet (v \bullet (x \bullet y)) \amp \text{ by I.H, because } |u| \lt |w| \\ \amp = (u \bullet v) \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{ by I.H, because } |u| \lt |w| \\ \amp = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \amp \text{Because } w = u \bullet v \end{align*}
    Therefore in all three cases we conclude that \((w \bullet x) \bullet y = w \bullet (x \bullet y) \, \blacksquare\)

    Subsection 15.1.5 Indices, Substrings annd Subsequences

    Finally, we'll conclude this section by formally defining several other common functions and terms related to strings.
    For any string \(w\) and any integer \(1 \leq i \leq |w|\text{,}\) the expression \(w_i\) denotes the \(i\)th symbol in \(w\text{,}\) counting from left to right (note: this text uses indexing starting at 1, not 0 like most programming languages). More formall, \(w_i\) is recursively defined as follows:
    \begin{equation*} w_i = \begin{cases} a & \text{if}\ w = ax \text{ and } i = 1 \\ x_{i-1} & \text{if}\ w = ax \text{ and } i \gt 1. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    As one might reasonably expect, \(w_i\) is formally undefined if \(i \lt 1\) or \(w = \epsilon\text{,}\) and therefore (by induction) if \(i \gt |w|\text{.}\) The integer \(i\) is called the index of \(w_i\text{.}\)
    We sometimes write strings as a concatenation of their constituent symbols using this subscript notation: \(w = w_1 w_2 \dots w_{|w|}\text{.}\) While completely standard, this notation is slightly misleading, because it incorrectly suggestst that the entire string \(w\) contains at least three symbols, when in fact \(w\) could be a single symbol or the empty string.
    In actual code, subscripts are usually expressed using the bracket notation \(w[i]\text{.}\) Brackets were introduced as a typographical convention over a hundred years ago because subscripts and superscripts were difficult or impossible to type. We sometimes write strings as explicit arrays \(w[1 .. n]\text{,}\) with the understanding that \(n = |w| \text{.}\) Again, this notation is potentially misleading; always remember that \(n\) might be zero; the string/array might be empty.
    A substringof a string \(w\) is another string obtained from \(w\) by deleting zero or more symbols from the beginning and from the end. Formally, a string \(y\) is a substring of \(w\) if and only if there are strings \(x\) and \(z\) such that \(w = xyz\text{.}\) Extending the array notation for strings, we write \(w [i .. j ]\) to denote the substring of \(w\) starting at \(w_i\) and ending at \(w_j\) . More formally, we define
    \begin{equation*} w[i..j] = \begin{cases} \epsilon & \text{if}\ j \lt i, \\ w_i \cdot w[i+1 .. j] & \text{otherwise}. \end{equation*}
